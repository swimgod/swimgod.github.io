![GM-Futurama-Exhibit](/assets/pictures/GM_Futurama_Exhibit.jpg){:class="img-responsive"}*1939: GM’s Futurama exhibit, an 18-minute ride carrying 500+ riders through a 1/3 mile course showcasing an automated highway system envisioned 20 years forward*

In 1939, the concept of autonomous vehicles first gained mainstream exposure at GM’s Futurama exhibit. The exhibit was a vision for 20 years into the future that contained an automated highway system and illustrated how the United States could be connected in a widespread network of highways and freeways.

![GM-RCA-Labs](/assets/pictures/GM_RCA_Lab_Testing.jpg){:class="img-responsive"}*Late-1950’s: GM and RCA Labs testing on slight left turn guided by embedded wires in the road*

In the next few decades, there were numerous forays to develop an infrastructure system that primarily revolved around radio-controlled cars propelled through electromagnetic fields embedded within the roads. In the 1950’s and 1960’s, GM partnered with RCA Labs to experiment using an electronic guide system that could autonomously glide down an automatic highway. This approach was expected to be commercialized by the 1970’s, but ultimately funding for these projects ended up being withdrawn.

![Stanford-Cart](/assets/pictures/Stanford_Cart.png){:class="img-responsive"}*1960’s: Stanford Cart began as a project to study the control of lunar rovers, but was re-designed to research visual navigation in autonomous vehicles*

Another notable experiment was the Stanford Cart, a buggy equipped with a video camera and remote control that had basic image processing capabilities and successfully navigated a room with several obstacles. Nevertheless, these early attempts represented the first steps toward autonomous driving as the vehicles increasingly gained capabilities.

![Tsukuba_Driverless_Vehicle](/assets/pictures/Tsukuba_Driverless_Vehicle.jpg){:class="img-responsive"}*1977: First computerized driverless vehicle capable of tracking lane markers built in Japan*

In the late 1970’s, Sadayuki Tsugawa built arguably the first truly autonomous vehicle capable of image processing on a road in Japan’s Tsukuba Mechanical Engineering Laboratory. Still, this project received input from an elevated rail.

![VaMoRs](/assets/pictures/VaMoRs.jpg){:class="img-responsive"}*1980’s: Ernst Dickmanns’ Mercedes van called the VaMoRs*

In the 1980’s to mid-1990’s, Ernst Dickmanns and his team worked on several projects implementing a vison-based system on a Mercedes S-Class called VaMoRs at Bundeswehr University Munich. His system was capable of detecting lane markings and helped to form the basis of computer vision (which he dubbed dynamic vision). The culmination of his work resulted in a successful trip of over 1,700 kilometers traveling ~95% of the distance autonomously reaching a maximum speed of 112mph in the Fall 1995! It is interesting to note that Ernst, who tested his vehicles on the Autobahn with no speed limit, placed an emphasis on speed, which marks a significant departure from our safety-first approach. Following his contributions to the space, Ernst was known as “the pioneer of the autonomous car.” Particularly, his contributions marked a shift away from cars guided by electromagnetic signals from embedded cables to the sensor-based systems that we use today. Late in the 1980’s, research efforts by several laboratories, universities and research institutes began using Lidar, neural networks and computer vision as well to control autonomous vehicles.

![nhaalogo_trans](/assets/pictures/nhaalogo_trans.gif){:class="img-responsive"}*1995: No Hands Across America Campaign by Carnegie Mellon*

Coming into the 2000’s, the U.S. Government spent a significant amount of money investing in unmanned vehicles into three military efforts called Demo I, Demo II and Demo III. While the basis of the technology had been established, DARPA (Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency) sought to accelerate the development of robotic cars that could both navigate obstacles as well as drive at reasonable speeds. This was the impetus for the first DARPA Grand Challenge in 2004 that encouraged contestants from around the world to compete for a $1 million cash prize. The goal of the first race was to navigate ~150 miles of desert roads in the Mojave Desert in California. In the inaugural competition, no vehicle traveled further than 8 miles, so there was no winner that year.

![Darpa_Challenge](/assets/pictures/Darpa_Challenge.jpg){:class="img-responsive"}*2005: Top 3 Finishers in 2nd DARPA Grand Challenge (Stanley, Sandstorm and Highlander)*

In 2005, the 2nd DARPA Grand Challenge was held and five cars successfully completed the ~130-mile course. Notably the Stanford team led by Sebastian Thrun won the competition followed by two Carnegie Mellon teams in the 2nd and 3rd spots.

![Darpa_Urban_Challenge](/assets/pictures/Darpa_Urban_Challenge.jpeg){:class="img-responsive"}*2007: Competing vehicles lined up for the Urban Challenge*

In 2007, the 3rd DARPA competition called the Urban Challenge was held that involved a difficult 60-mile race in an urban setting. During this competition, the vehicles also had to obey traffic rules. This time around, Carnegie Mellon came out on top claiming the $2 million prize with Stanford coming in 2nd.

![Moores_Law](/assets/pictures/Moores_Law.jpeg){:class="img-responsive"}*Representation of compute capability with Nvidia leading the way into the future. It is also important to note that Moore’s Law began prior to IC era*

The last seven decades saw steady advancements in autonomous driving technology leading up to the influx of incumbents and start-ups building self-driving systems in the last 10 years. Within the last decade, much of what has allowed the rapid pace of innovation is Moore’s Law, which is an observed effect that the number of transistors on a computer chip doubles every 18-24 months resulting in exponentially increasing computing power. This is a major catalyst driving significant improvements in AI, computer vision and beyond. Skipping ahead into the present day, all the major automotive OEMs are involved in one way or another in building/investing in an autonomous vehicle system, so hopefully this article helps you better understand the technological advances leading up to the vibrant autonomous ecosystem we have today.
