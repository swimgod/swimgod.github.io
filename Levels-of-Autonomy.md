**Who defined the autonomous vehicle levels?**

The Society of Automotive Engineers (SAE) International, an organization with automotive experts around the world, developed a classification system for defining driving automation for motor vehicles in January 2014. This classification system for driver-less cars was adopted by the U.S. Development of Transportation and the United Nations.

**Level 0: No Driving Automation**

At Level 0, the vehicle has no autonomous vehicle controls. The driver controls the steering, throttle, accelerating, braking and navigating. Much of the cars today are at Level 0. This level does include warning systems such as blind-spot indicators or collision warnings).

**Level 1: Driver Assistance**

At Level 1, the vehicle has steering, throttling, accelerating and braking capabilities. Level 1 vehicles do not take over both acceleration/deceleration and steering functions at once. However, the driver controls the critical driving tasks including taking over these functions when the vehicle notifies the driver. Examples of driver assistance systems at Level 1 are Adaptive Cruise Control or Lane Correction.

**Level 2: Partial Driving Automation**

At Level 2, the vehicle has steering, throttling, accelerating and braking capabilities. The caveat is that the vehicle is able to take over both steering and acceleration/deceleration capabilities. The driver is still responsible of monitoring surroundings and must be ready to take over control of the vehicle at any time. A key example of Level 2 is Tesla’s Enhanced Autopilot.

**Level 3: Conditional Automated Driving**

At Level 3, the vehicle controls all monitoring of the environment, signifying a shift of “safety-critical” functions from the driver to the vehicle under certain traffic and environmental conditions. The driver must still be expected to monitor surroundings and take over control as needed. Level 3 has been at the center of controversy because drivers will not realistically remain alert when they are only required to monitor passively. Therefore, many autonomous driving companies intend to skip Level 3 to Level 4 (i.e. Ford).

**Level 4: High Automated Driving**

At Level 4, the vehicle requires no driver interaction in a wider range of environments. The vehicles will still include functional driving equipment (i.e. steering wheel, pedals) for any conditions outside of the vehicle’s “operational design domain”, such as severe conditions. When the autonomous driving system is safe to use, the vehicle would notify the driver and would not require the driver after that. If the autonomous system fails at any point, the vehicle is able to bring itself to a stop.

**Level 5: Full Automated Driving**

At Level 5, the vehicle is completely autonomous. The vehicle will no longer include functional driving equipment (i.e. steering wheel, pedals), but it is not expected to operate in extreme conditions in the near future. The driver will only have to set the destination and the vehicle will control the rest. If the autonomous system fails at any point, the vehicle is able to bring itself to a stop.
